**Date:** March 2026
**Prepared for:** Agent + family contact — 7-hour drive visit planning
**Bottom line up front:** Three homes are worth the trip. Two are in Stow. One is in Hudson. They can be seen in a single day. Everything else on the list has been eliminated by the data or the family's own criteria.

---

## Who They Are and What They Actually Need

Before the rankings mean anything, this context changes the math significantly:

**The family:**
- Two adults, both working fully from home (commute to work = irrelevant)
- Two daughters: 6 years old and 7 months old
- Currently living comfortably in a 1,500 sqft main floor (with a 1,500 sqft basement they rarely use — it's storage and occasional office/play space, not daily living)

**What the 4th bedroom actually means:**
Both parents work from home. The "4th bedroom" on this search is not a guest room or a future kid's room — it is a dedicated home office. Without it, someone is working from the kitchen table or a shared space. That matters for day-to-day livability but it is a different priority than a bedroom for a child.

**The bathroom reality:**
The family has already assessed this themselves: one bathroom works for roughly the next 3 years. The 7-month-old won't be competing for bathroom time. The 6-year-old and two adults can manage on a shared schedule. The 1-bathroom constraint is real but time-limited by their own evaluation.

**Space calibration:**
They currently live well in 1,500 sqft. Any home at or above that number is equivalent to what they already know works. The 1,800 sqft target is aspirational, not a hard floor.

**What the wife wants most:**
Fast payoff. Close to the school. She does not want a tenant in the home — rental income from an on-property suite is off the table.

**What the husband is open to:**
Everything above, plus the tenant option if the financial case is strong enough. For this document, tenant income is not counted in any calculation — it's upside if they change their minds.

**The goal:**
Own the home free and clear within 5 years. The driver is not impatience — it is AI-driven job displacement risk. One or both of their positions could be automated or eliminated within that window. A paid-off home in that scenario is the difference between a crisis and an inconvenience.

## Home #1 to Visit: Charring Cross Dr, Stow

### The Financial Case Is Unmatched

After putting $25K down and recasting with $180K, the remaining loan balance is **$14,900**. The first December bonus — $30,000 — is nearly double that amount. This home is paid off in approximately one year from closing. Total interest paid over the life of the loan: **~$900**. Not per month. Total.

That is the 5-year debt-free goal achieved in year one, leaving four full years of financial runway before the AI threat window even closes.

At payoff, monthly housing cost drops to **$433/mo** (tax + insurance only). That number is sustainable on a significantly reduced salary, on a part-time income, or as a bridge on savings. It is genuine financial security.

### Does It Work for the Family?

**Space:** 1,591 sqft. They currently live well in 1,500 sqft. This is more house than they use every day right now.

**Bedrooms:** 3 bedrooms. The math: parents in one room, 6-year-old in one room, 7-month-old in the parents' room for now. That leaves one room as a home office — one office, not two. This is a real constraint. For the first year or two, one parent has a dedicated office and the other uses a shared space or the living room. It's not ideal but it's not unique either — millions of people work from home without a dedicated office. More critically, once the mortgage is gone at the end of year one, there's cash to add on, convert space, or simply not care because the financial pressure is gone.

**Bathrooms:** 1 bathroom. The family has evaluated this themselves and concluded it works for ~3 years. The 7-month-old adds no bathroom competition. The 6-year-old and two adults on a morning schedule is manageable. This is a real inconvenience, not a dealbreaker, given the timeline.

**Basement:** None. This is the one genuine structural gap versus what they have now. No storm shelter, no extra storage, no overflow space. Ohio weather makes the no-basement situation worth noting.

**Location:** Stow, 14 min to school. Stow is a desirable suburb — familiar community, access to Akron amenities, no HOA.

**Comps:** Six comparable Stow sales ranged $179,500–$270,000. Charring Cross is the largest home in that comp set at the second-lowest price. Four of the six smaller comparable homes sold for more. The market supports the price.

**Condition:** Personal rating 8/10 condition, 9/10 potential. The family has already looked at this one and liked what they saw.

**Just listed:** No DOM history, no prior seller behavior to read. Come in with a fair offer — the comps don't support aggressive lowballing, but the asking price is already at the low end of the comp range.

### The Honest Tradeoff

One bathroom and one home office (not two) for a period measured in months, in exchange for being mortgage-free before the next year is out. The 1962 build means an inspection is non-negotiable — verify the mechanicals, roof, and electrical panel condition before any offer.

### What to Look At During the Showing

- Inspect the bathroom carefully — condition, size, whether a second bath could ever be added
- Look at the layout for a second office solution (finished basement potential? No. But could a room be subdivided? Could one parent work from the living room or kitchen area comfortably?)
- Age of roof, HVAC, water heater
- No basement — ask about drainage, grading, and where the family would shelter in a storm
- Garage condition and whether it's insulated/heated (usable workspace in Ohio winters)

## Home #2 to Visit: Fishcreek Rd, Stow

### The Balanced Choice

Fishcreek solves almost everything Charring Cross doesn't, at a moderate financial cost. Four bedrooms means dedicated offices for both parents today, with room left for the girls as they grow. Two full bathrooms means no morning scheduling puzzle. The MIL suite — even without a tenant — is a significant asset for this family.

Paid off in **~2 years** in the best case. Total interest: **~$2,800**. Best-case monthly: **$732/mo**.

### The MIL Suite Changes the Work-From-Home Picture

The suite has its own entrance, its own kitchen, its own bathroom, its own deck, and independent utility meters. For a family where both adults work from home, this is not a rental unit — it is a **completely separate workspace**. One parent takes the suite as a full home office. The other takes one of the four main bedrooms. The girls have their own rooms. Everyone has separation during the workday, which is something no other home on this list can offer at any price.

Yes, the wife prefers not to have a stranger living there. That's fully respected — this calculation doesn't include rental income at all. But the suite as a private office for one of them is a genuine quality-of-life upgrade that makes working from home more sustainable long-term.

### Does It Work for the Family?

**Space:** 1,666 sqft main house + the suite. The effective daily living space is more than the listed number.

**Bedrooms:** 4. Parents in one, 6-year-old in one, baby eventually in one, and one dedicated office from day one. The suite adds a fifth workspace. This is the most comfortable bedroom/office setup on the list.

**Bathrooms:** 2 full baths in the main house + 1 in the suite. No bathroom bottleneck at any stage.

**Lot:** 0.65 acres — the largest lot reviewed. For two young girls, this matters more in a few years than it does now.

**Location:** Stow, 12 min to school. Same neighborhood context as Charring Cross, slightly closer to school.

**Comps:** All five comparable Stow sales priced above Fishcreek — the weakest was $250,000 for a home 186 sqft smaller. The strongest was $310,000 for a home 334 sqft smaller. Fishcreek is underpriced relative to its market. Value is real.

**98 DOM:** This is leverage. Two price cuts already ($260,000 → $244,500). A seller who has been watching the phone not ring for 98 days is a seller who will negotiate. Ask directly: why hasn't it sold? The answer matters.

**1953 build:** Oldest home reviewed. A thorough inspection is mandatory, not optional. Focus specifically on: electrical panel age and capacity, plumbing (original cast iron vs. updated), HVAC system age, roof, and foundation. The cape cod style may have some quirky upper-floor ceiling angles — verify the bedroom dimensions on the second floor are usable.

**Personal score 62% / gut feel 4/10:** This is the flag on Fishcreek. The numbers favor it strongly but something about it didn't resonate emotionally when reviewed. That reaction might change when standing in it — or it might not. The showing will answer this.

### What to Look At During the Showing

- Walk the suite first — picture it as a private office, not a rental unit. Does it feel separate enough from the main house for a productive workday?
- Upper floor bedroom dimensions — cape cod homes can have tight ceilings on the second story
- The 0.65 acre lot — walk the property, understand what's usable vs. wooded or sloped
- Inspect the 1953 mechanicals specifically: electrical panel, water heater, furnace age
- Condition of the second bathroom — is it updated or original?
- Why has it sat 98 days? Ask the listing agent directly

## Home #3 to Visit: Ogilby Dr, Hudson

### The Home They Actually Want

Ogilby scored 90% personal and 81% combined — the highest of any home reviewed on both measures. The family's gut reaction to this house was clear. Four bedrooms, 2.5 baths, 2,016 sqft, 9-minute commute, full basement, wood-burning fireplace, deck, patio, Hudson location. It checks every objective box and it's the one they liked most.

**The comps are extraordinary.** Every comparable Hudson sale closed between $425,000 and $480,000. Ogilby is listed at $375,000 — $50,000 to $105,000 below every comparable home. This is not a rounding error. The market data says you are buying a $430,000 home for $375,000. Built-in equity from day one.

### The Financial Reality, Stated Plainly

The best-case monthly payment is **$1,555/mo**. The loan remaining after the recast is **$170,000**. With December bonuses hitting every year, payoff takes approximately **5 years** — right at the edge of the goal with no margin for a missed bonus, a disrupted income, or an unexpected expense.

The worst case (no bonuses, standard recast) is a **7-year payoff** at **$2,072/mo**.

If AI disruption hits in year 2 and the December bonus disappears, you are making a $1,555/mo payment on a $130,000 remaining balance with no clear end in sight. That is a survivable but stressful position. It requires both incomes to stay largely intact to execute the plan.

### Why It's Still on the List

Two reasons:

**First, the equity cushion is a real safety net.** If circumstances force a sale at year 2 or 3, comps suggest you sell for $430,000+. You'd recover all $205,000 committed at closing and likely more. No other home on this list offers that. The equity isn't cash in hand, but it is an exit that doesn't hurt you.

**Second, for this family specifically, the 4-bedroom + 2.5-bath + basement configuration matters more than it would for another buyer.** Both parents work from home full-time. Having two dedicated offices, individual bedrooms for two growing girls, and 2.5 baths for the rest of the time you own this home — these are not luxury preferences. They're practical needs for a household where four people are in the house together all day, every day.

Charring Cross solves the financial problem and forces daily compromises on space and office separation. Ogilby solves the daily living problem and requires the finances to execute perfectly for 5 years.

### The Honest Ask

Before scheduling this showing, the couple should agree on one question: **if both jobs survive intact and both bonuses land every year, is $1,555/mo for 5 years acceptable?** If yes, Ogilby goes on the list and the showing is worth having. If either person has genuine doubt about sustaining $1,555/mo through disruption, skip this showing — it will be harder to walk away from once you've been inside.

### What to Look At During the Showing

- The basement: listed as full and unfinished. Get a real sense of what finishing it would cost and what it could become — it changes the long-term livability of the home significantly
- The 1974 build: similar age concern to Fishcreek but less extreme. Inspect HVAC, roof, electrical
- The aluminum siding: check for dents, fading, and seal around windows — aluminum siding is low maintenance but has a ceiling on visual appeal
- Just listed at $375,000 (previously sold at $378,000 in 2022): no negotiating history yet. The comp data suggests the asking price is already below fair market — aggressive lowballing is unlikely to land

Even if the price comes down to $310,000, you're buying in a location where the market has demonstrated resistance at multiple price points.

## Decision Framework for After the Visit

After seeing all three, the choice will likely come down to one question: **how confident are you in both incomes for the next 5 years?**

- **High confidence, want the best house:** Ogilby. The comps support it, you're buying equity, and the 5-year plan is executable if the bonuses land.
- **Moderate confidence, want balance of livability and safety:** Fishcreek. Two offices, two baths, four bedrooms, 2-year payoff, MIL suite as insurance if needed later.
- **Conservative, want maximum financial security:** Charring Cross. Paid off in one year. $433/mo housing cost at payoff. One bathroom for ~3 years and one shared office space — temporary constraints in exchange for permanent financial independence.

The agent should know that the family is not buying based on emotion. They have data on every home and have thought through the financial implications carefully. The right showing approach is to let the homes speak for themselves and answer the questions above honestly.
