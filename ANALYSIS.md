**Goal:** Own the home free and clear within 5 years
**Why 5 years:** AI-driven career disruption risk makes financial security a hard deadline, not a preference. If one or both incomes are reduced or eliminated, a paid-off home transforms a crisis into a manageable situation.

**Financial assumptions across all rankings:**
- $25,000 cash down at closing

The core question for every home is not *"Which house do I like best?"* but *"Which house gets us to zero the fastest, with the most survivable monthly payment if income drops?"*

## Ranking Logic

Before going home by home, the framework:

**Speed to zero beats everything else.** The 5-year deadline is driven by fear of income loss — which means the longer a mortgage stays open, the longer both incomes are *required*. A home paid off in 1 year leaves 4 years of financial runway to survive disruption. A home paid off in 5 years leaves none.

**The floor payment is your survival number.** Best-case monthly payment is what you owe if everything goes right. That number is also what you'll owe if the Dec bonus disappears in a bad year. The lower that floor, the more resilient the household.

**Comps are a risk signal, not just a number.** A home priced below its comps builds equity from day one and provides an exit option. A home priced above comps, or one the market has already rejected, traps you.

**Ongoing costs don't disappear at payoff.** Property tax and HOA are forever. A $7,400/yr tax bill is $617/mo you owe for the rest of your life, whether you have a mortgage or not.

## #1 — 3846 Charring Cross Dr, Stow OH

**$219,900 · 3 bed / 1 bath · 1,591 sqft · 14 min commute**
**Best case: $522/mo · $14,900 loan remaining · ~1 year payoff · ~$900 total interest**

Not $900 per month — $900 total.

That is the financial outcome you're trying to achieve. Everything else is noise.

### Financial Case

The $522/mo best-case payment breaks down as $89 P&I, $333 tax, $100 insurance. That means even in a worst-case income scenario, $522/mo keeps a roof over your family's head. Compare that to a median rent in this area of $1,400–$1,800/mo for equivalent space — the owned home wins decisively once paid off, and the path to payoff is measured in months, not years.

At payoff, your ongoing housing cost drops to $433/mo (tax + insurance only). That number is achievable on part-time work, on a significantly reduced salary, or even as a bridge period on savings. That is genuine financial security.

The comps support the price. Six comparable Stow sales ranged from $179,500 to $270,000. Charring Cross is the largest home in that set at the second-lowest price. Four of the six comparable homes sold for *more* despite being smaller. The market is telling you this house is priced fairly or slightly below fair.

### The Real Concern: 1 Bathroom

This is the only legitimate argument against ranking this #1, and it deserves honest treatment. One bathroom for four people — two adults, presumably children — is a daily friction point. Morning routines, sick days, guests, five years of this reality.

The counter-argument: you are buying five years of time, not a forever home. The debt-free outcome at the end of year one transforms your options entirely. With no mortgage, a $30K bonus has no mandatory destination. A bathroom addition (estimated $15,000–$25,000) could be completed in year two, paid in cash. The total cost of the house plus a bathroom addition would likely still come in under what you'd spend on any other home on this list.

The 3-bedroom shortfall follows similar logic — a bedroom addition or a finished space in the future is fundable from cash flow after the mortgage is gone.

### Livability for 5 Years

- Stow location: 14 min to school, good access to Akron amenities
- 2-car attached garage, patio, vinyl siding (low maintenance)
- Gas forced air heat with central A/C — reliable, cheap to operate
- No HOA, no basement (no storm shelter — worth noting for Ohio)
- Just listed at $219,900 vs. prior sale of $200,000 — limited upside in negotiation, but fair pricing
- Personal gut feel: 8/10 condition, 9/10 potential — the family sees something here

### Verdict

If the 5-year debt-free goal is real and not aspirational, this is the answer. The inconveniences are real but temporary and fixable. The financial freedom is permanent.

## #6 — 5866 Ogilby Dr, Hudson OH

**$375,000 · 4 bed / 2.5 bath · 2,016 sqft · 9 min commute**
**Best case: $1,555/mo · $170,000 loan remaining · ~5 years payoff · ~$30K total interest**
**Worst case: $2,072/mo · ~7 year payoff**

### The Hardest Ranking to Write

Ogilby is the best house on this list. It checks every objective box: 4 bedrooms, 2.5 baths, 2,016 sqft, best commute at 9 minutes, full basement with finishing potential, wood-burning fireplace, deck, patio. The personal score is 90% — the highest of any home reviewed. And crucially, the comps are extraordinary: every comparable Hudson sale landed between $425,000 and $480,000. This home is listed at $375,000 — $50,000 to $105,000 below every comparable. On paper, you're buying a $430K home for $375K.

So why is it #6?

### The 5-Year Math Doesn't Work

The remaining loan after the $25K down and $180K recast is $170,000. That is four times the Charring Cross loan, four times the Fishcreek loan, nearly four times the Lovers Lane loan. With $30K/yr December bonuses, payoff takes approximately 5 years — right at the edge of the goal, with no margin.

If income is disrupted in year 2 or 3 — the exact scenario this ranking is designed to protect against — you are still carrying a $100K+ mortgage. The best-case monthly payment of $1,555 is the highest of any home ranked 1 through 6. If one income disappears and the bonus disappears with it, you are making $1,555/mo payments on a house that still has $150,000+ on the loan. That is not a survivable position on a single middle-income salary.

### The Equity Argument

The one genuine counterargument for Ogilby: the $50-100K gap between asking price and comps means equity is built from day one. If something forces a sale in year 2 or 3, you would likely sell for $425K+ — recovering your down payment, your recast, and showing a meaningful gain. The other homes on this list don't offer that.

But equity is only realizable when you sell. A family that needs the home to *stay lived in* doesn't benefit from paper equity during a cash-flow crisis. And Ogilby's high monthly payment is a cash-flow problem, not an equity problem.

### The Honest Answer

If the 5-year debt-free goal is firm, Ogilby is risky. If the goal is flexible — "we'd *like* to be debt-free in 5 years" — Ogilby becomes much more interesting, especially given the personal score of 90% and the comps backing. If both jobs survive AI disruption and both bonuses land, you're debt-free at year 5 on the best house for less than its market value. That's a great outcome. The risk is that this scenario requires everything to go right.

### Verdict

Best house. Wrong goal alignment. Ranked #6 not because it's a bad choice — it may be the objectively best home on this list — but because it is the riskiest choice relative to the 5-year debt-free objective. If the income risk ever feels less acute, revisit this one first.

## #7 — 9431 Briar Dr, Streetsboro OH

**$274,800 · 3 bed / 1 bath · 1,836 sqft · 12 min commute**
**Best case: $1,137/mo · $69,800 loan remaining · ~3 years payoff · ~$6,700 total interest**
**Worst case: $1,684/mo · ~5 year payoff**

### The Tax Problem

Briar Drive has a single disqualifying number: $7,427/yr in property tax ($619/mo). That figure is not a mortgage payment — it is a permanent government obligation that exists whether you have a mortgage or not, for as long as you own the home.

To understand what that means: Beaver Trail, the home ranked just above Briar, has a property tax of $3,751/yr ($313/mo). The difference between the two homes' taxes is $3,676/yr — $306/mo — *every year, forever*. Over a 10-year horizon after payoff, Briar costs $36,760 more in taxes alone than Beaver Trail. Over 20 years: $73,520. That is a second mortgage hidden inside the tax bill.

The best-case monthly payment for Briar is $1,137/mo, but $619 of that — 54% — is property tax and never goes away. After payoff, your housing cost drops to $719/mo (tax + insurance). Compare that to Charring Cross at $433/mo or Beaver Trail at $467/mo after payoff.

For a family pursuing financial security against income risk, an inescapable $719/mo housing floor even after achieving "debt freedom" is a serious structural problem.

### Other Concerns

Beyond the tax, Briar has practical livability issues:
- 1 bathroom for a family of 4 — same problem as Charring Cross, but Charring Cross has dramatically better financial numbers
- No basement — no storm shelter in Ohio, no expansion space
- 1957 build under renovation cosmetics — the renovation makes it look good but doesn't change the age of what's behind the walls
- Personal gut feel: 3/10 — the family already senses something is off

The renovation is appealing, but cosmetic improvements don't change tax assessments, bathroom counts, or build quality underneath. The 1,836 sqft does meet the space target — the only 3-bedroom home on the list that does.

### One Legitimate Positive

The 3-year payoff timeline is the same as Beaver Trail, and the commute at 12 minutes is good. If the tax somehow came down (unlikely without a formal appeal, and current assessment suggests it won't), Briar would be a better option. Verify the tax figure before even scheduling a showing — if it's an error, recalculate everything.

### Verdict

The property tax burden makes Briar a worse long-term financial choice than the homes ranked above it, even though the payoff timeline is competitive. Ranked #7 because the perpetual post-payoff cost undermines the financial security goal.

- **Need 4 bedrooms + income resilience hedge?** → Fishcreek (#2)

**Homes #7 and #8 should not be purchased given the stated goals.** Briar's tax burden and Sapphire's overpricing create financial risks that don't resolve even after the mortgage is gone.
